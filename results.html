The time trials were used to evaluate our robot under the final course scoring rules. Our goal was not only to achieve a fast run, but also to demonstrate that the robot could complete the course consistently using the sensing, encoder feedback, and control logic developed throughout the project.

Based on the official attempts, our best counted result was 120.00 seconds. We also recorded an additional full run of 119.31 seconds, but that run was marked unofficial. Overall, the data shows that the robot was capable of finishing the course near the two-minute mark, but repeatability remained one of the main challenges.

Summary of Performance

Trial 4: Successful full run in 119.31 s, but not counted as official.

One useful observation from the successful runs is that the middle checkpoint times were very consistent. Trial 1 and Trial 4 both reached checkpoint 3 at about 85.2 seconds and checkpoint 4 at 100.10 seconds. This suggests that once the robot settled into the course properly, its path following and state transitions were reasonably repeatable.

The larger issue was reliability across attempts. Two of the three official runs did not progress beyond the early portion of the course, which means our design could produce a full completion but still needed improved robustness. In other words, the robot had the speed needed for a competitive result, but not yet the consistency needed for every run.

Control Discussion

Our best motor-control performance came from using Kp = 2.9, Ki = 0.1, motor gain K = 0.2, and a setpoint of 120 counts/s. These values gave the most stable balance between response speed and tracking accuracy during the final testing phase.

A major challenge during tuning was that small gain changes often produced noticeably different behavior. In some cases, slight adjustments caused the robot to become less stable or less repeatable, especially during transitions between sections of the course. Because of this sensitivity, tuning became a tradeoff between achieving fast motion and maintaining enough stability to finish the run.

The results page reflects that tradeoff clearly. The robot demonstrated that it could complete the course in about two minutes, but the DNF trials show that future improvement should focus on making performance more repeatable rather than only trying to reduce the final time.
